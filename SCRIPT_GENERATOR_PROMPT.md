Every day, Pip the Otter runs one whimsical experiment in his magical workshop. ## 1. The Character (never deviate)

**Pip** is a small, chubby river otter inventor.

- **Body**: soft warm-brown fur with a felted, claymation-like texture; lighter tan muzzle, chest and belly; short rounded limbs; thick tapered tail; small ears; black whiskers; round dark chocolate-brown eyes; small dark brown nose.
- **Outfit (always worn, never changes)**: round **teal goggles** with a dark strap, worn over his eyes; a **mustard-yellow utility vest** with two front flap pockets, a tiny zipper pull, and a **small red heart** stitched on the left chest.
- **Personality**: boundlessly curious, easily delighted, a little clumsy, never mean or scared for long. He treats every experiment like the greatest event in history. When surprised, his eyes go wide and his paws fly up. He giggles.
- **Voice — Pip is ALWAYS non-verbal**: he never pronounces words in any language. The ONLY syllables he may voice are his signature **"Pip!"** and **"Pip-pip!"** squeaks — everything else is natural wordless otter sound: happy squeals, sharp little intakes of breath when surprised, giggles, chirps, delighted trills. CRITICAL — how to write his sounds so the video model performs them instead of speaking them:
  - Quotation marks may ONLY ever contain "Pip!" or "Pip-pip!" — the video model lip-syncs any quoted text literally.
  - Every other sound must be written as a described performance: *"Pip lets out a high-pitched happy squeal"*, *"a sharp little intake of breath, eyes going wide"* — NEVER as a quoted or named sound like "gasp!", "wow!", "yay!", or the model will make Pip literally pronounce those words.
  - NEVER write vocalizations as a list or fixed sequence (no "Pip-pip!, a gasp, a cheer" chains). Give Pip at most 1–2 natural vocal moments, placed where they genuinely fit, and vary them from day to day.
  - His expressions and body language carry the emotion (wide eyes, paws to cheeks, happy bounce).

**Hard rules**: Pip is the ONLY character. No humans, no other animals, no crowds. He never gets hurt. Never remove or alter his goggles, vest, or heart patch. He NEVER speaks words — otter noises only. He NEVER holds a magic wand, stick, or handheld magical object: he operates his machines exclusively through their built-in levers, cranks, buttons, and dials.

## 2. The Setting (never deviate)

Pip's **cozy magical workshop**: a warm clay-rendered room with soft tan/sand-colored walls and floor, a rounded arched window glowing with warm golden light, a small wooden shelf with books and jars, and a simple wooden stool. Faint **purple-violet magical sparkle wisps** drift through the air. Lighting is always warm amber, soft and cozy, with a gentle glow from the experiment itself.

## 3. The Machines

Pip's flagship tool is **The Mystical Press**: a chunky, toy-like **plum-purple hydraulic press** with rounded edges, two ball finials on top, a **glowing golden star emblem** on the crossbar, a dark coiled spring above the round upper platen, a **glowing amber pedestal plate** where objects sit, and **exactly ONE lever** — mounted on its right side, with a glowing amber orb handle. That single side lever is the only control; there is never a second lever, and never anything lever-like on top. Pip pulls it DOWN once to run a press cycle. When the press works, it hums, hisses softly, and releases swirls of purple magic.

The press is the star, but Pip may instead use another workshop machine, always rendered in the **same plum-purple + glowing-amber toy aesthetic**: a vacuum chamber (glass dome), a shrink/grow ray, a freeze chamber, a giant horseshoe magnet, a mixer/whisk contraption, a slicer, a magnifier beam, a bubble machine, etc. Think of the popular "satisfying experiment" genre (hydraulic press channels, vacuum chamber tests, freeze-dry, magnet crushes) — but magical, cute, and G-rated.

**Machine physics rules (strict)**:
- **The Mystical Press PRESSES — it never transmutes.** An object placed in the press must still be that same object (or its squished/altered remains) when the press lifts. It can flatten, deform, crack, crumble, shatter into sparkly pieces, ooze, stretch, wobble back into shape, glow, erupt with a cool magical reaction, or produce a surprising *physical* result — but it must NEVER disappear and become a different object. (A pressed crayon can become a rainbow puddle; it cannot become a butterfly.)
- **How the press moves (describe this explicitly in the video prompt)**: the object sits still on the glowing amber pedestal; the upper platen descends slowly and STRAIGHT DOWN onto it, presses with visible force while the object **visibly compresses and deforms under the contact pressure** — this slow satisfying squish IS the core of the video — then the platen rises straight back up, leaving the result on the pedestal. **The platen starts FULLY RAISED**: at 0:00 there is clear open space between the raised platen and the whole, untouched object — the video must NEVER begin with the platen lowered, the object already partially pressed or deformed, or the object tucked under/behind any part of the machine. **Exactly ONE press cycle per video**: down once, squeeze, up once — never a second press, never re-pressing. The press never lifts, grabs, attracts, or picks up the object; the object never sticks to the platen, never floats, and never reacts before the platen is actually pressing on it. Any magical effect happens *because of* the squeeze, during or after contact — not on mere touch.
- **Every effect belongs to the OBJECT, never the machine**: the payoff — eruptions, sparkles, particles, color bursts, transformations — must visibly originate FROM the object on the pedestal (squished cookies erupt their own sprinkles; a pressed geode spills its own crystals). The machine itself only moves, hums, hisses, and glows its ambient purple — it NEVER spontaneously sprays, spews, or emits confetti, sparkles, liquids, or anything else. A concept like "the machine celebrates by shooting confetti" is wrong; "the object bursts into confetti when squeezed" is right.
- **Exception — the object may affect the MACHINE when it makes cause-and-effect sense**: an especially strong or mystical object can comically overwhelm the equipment — the press strains and bends, its spring boings loose, the platen wobbles helplessly; a too-powerful object can crack or shatter the vacuum chamber's glass dome. This must always be the OBJECT causing a consequence TO the machine (never the machine acting on its own), and the damage stays cute and comical — cartoon-style boings, wobbles, and pops, never scary destruction. Pip's flabbergasted reaction to his machine misbehaving is great comedy.
- Genuine transformations (one object becoming another) are allowed **only** with machines whose whole premise is transformation (the shrink/grow ray, etc.) — never the press. If the concept's payoff doesn't come from *compression*, don't use the press — pick the machine whose mechanism actually produces the payoff (vacuum chamber, freeze chamber, magnet, etc.).
- **Every machine is operated mechanically**: Pip pulls the crank lever, pushes big chunky buttons, or turns dials. Machines never activate by magic gesture, and Pip never uses a wand.

## 4. Visual Style (must appear in every video prompt)

Soft 3D claymation / felted-clay render, rounded toy-like shapes, matte textures, warm amber lighting, gentle purple magical particle wisps, shallow cozy depth, **vertical 9:16**, smooth slow camera (static or gentle slow push-in — never shaky, never fast cuts). Everything looks huggable.

## 5. Format Rules (AI-video constraints — strict)

- **Exactly one continuous shot**, ~8 seconds. No cuts, no scene changes, no time skips.
- **One simple action** with a clear beginning → payoff. (Machine works on object → surprising/delightful result.)
- **Start mid-action — zero setup time.** Frame one: the object is already in/on the machine, Pip is already at the controls with his paw on the lever/button, visibly excited. He never walks in, carries the object over, or prepares anything. Every second of screen time belongs to the experiment itself.
- **Beat timing for the 8 seconds**: **0–1s** — the object sits whole, intact, and fully visible in the machine (press: platen fully raised with clear space above the untouched object), machine glowing in anticipation, and Pip immediately pulls the lever; **1–5s** — the machine runs ONE single cycle: the star section, the slow satisfying squish/reaction shown in loving detail from completely intact to fully transformed, every stage visible on screen (never hidden behind or inside the machine); **5–8s** — the payoff lands and Pip reacts with visible delight. Prefer endings that **loop well** back into the opening frame.
- **Vocal moments**: give Pip 1–2 natural vocal touches per video, freely placed and varied day to day — do NOT default to the same sounds in the same order every episode (see section 1 for how to write them).
- **Payoff must land**: a comedic surprise, a shower of sparkles, an absurdly wrong-but-adorable physical result.
- **Vocalizations, not dialogue**: Pip never speaks words. Quotation marks are reserved for his only voiced syllables — "Pip!" and "Pip-pip!" — every other sound (squeal, breath-intake, trill, giggle) must be described as a performed action/sound, never quoted and never named as a word to be said (quoted text gets spoken literally by the video model). Never chain sounds into a fixed sequence.
- **Sound design is half the video**: always specify it — hydraulic hisses, satisfying squishes/crackles/pops (ASMR-style), magical chimes, a soft musical sting on the payoff, Pip's giggle.
- **Never include**: on-screen text or captions, readable labels/logos/brand text (trend items must be recognizable by shape and color only), more than one character or creature of ANY size (Pip is the only living being; even a toy/plush/figurine object must stay completely inanimate — it never stands, moves, or reacts on its own), spoken words from Pip, wands or handheld magical objects, small intricate objects requiring fine detail, anything scary, gross, or violent. Objects can squish, crumble, react, or sparkle — never break in a distressing way.
